Procurement & Sourcing

📘 Deep Dive · Week 1 · Procurement
Letters of Credit (LC) — Complete Guide
A Letter of Credit is a bank-issued document guaranteeing a seller receives payment, provided they fulfil the conditions stated in the LC. It is one of the most critical instruments in trade finance and international procurement. Understanding the types of LC helps you choose the right instrument for every transaction.
Type 01
Revocable LC
Can be amended or cancelled by the issuing bank at any time without prior notice to the beneficiary (seller).

Risk: Very high risk for seller — almost never used in modern trade.
When used: Only when buyer and seller have very high mutual trust.
Practical NoteAs a buyer, you have maximum flexibility. As a seller, avoid accepting this — you have no guarantee of payment.
Type 02
Irrevocable LC
Cannot be cancelled or modified without the consent of ALL parties — issuing bank, confirming bank, and beneficiary.

Risk: Low for seller. Standard in international trade.
When used: Almost all modern international procurement uses Irrevocable LC as the baseline.
Practical NoteThis is your default choice when dealing with new or unknown suppliers internationally. It protects both parties and is accepted by all major banks globally.
Type 03
Confirmed LC
An irrevocable LC that is additionally guaranteed by a second bank (usually in the seller's country).

Risk: Minimum risk for seller — two banks guarantee payment.
When used: When the issuing bank's country has political or financial instability.
Practical NoteIf you're sourcing from a country where your supplier doesn't trust the buyer's banking system, offering a Confirmed LC removes the barrier and gets you better terms.
Type 04
Standby LC
Acts as a backup guarantee — payment is only triggered if the buyer fails to fulfil their obligation. Similar in concept to a Bank Guarantee.

Risk: Seller is protected only if buyer defaults.
When used: Long-term supply contracts, services agreements, infrastructure projects.
Practical NoteUse Standby LC with trusted suppliers in ongoing contracts where you want to provide security without going through full LC documentation for every shipment.
Type 05
Revolving LC
Automatically reinstates itself for the same amount after each use — eliminating the need to open a new LC for every shipment.

Risk: Low — same terms apply to all shipments.
When used: Regular, repeated purchases from the same supplier (monthly orders, scheduled deliveries).
Practical NoteIf you order from the same supplier every month, a Revolving LC saves significant bank charges and administrative effort compared to opening a new LC each time.
Type 06
Transferable LC
Allows the first beneficiary (seller) to transfer part or all of the credit to a second beneficiary — typically the actual manufacturer or sub-supplier.

Risk: Moderate — depends on second beneficiary's performance.
When used: When your supplier is a trading company, not the manufacturer.
Practical NoteIf your supplier sources from a factory and needs to pass your payment security to that factory, a Transferable LC enables the entire chain to work smoothly.
🎯 Key Takeaway for Your Team The choice of LC type is a negotiation tool — not just a banking formality. Use Irrevocable LC as your default. Upgrade to Confirmed LC when country risk is high. Use Revolving LC to save cost on repeat orders. Never accept a Revocable LC as a seller. Understanding these differences puts you in a stronger position in every international sourcing conversation.

Logistics & Supply Chain

📘 Deep Dive · Week 1 · Logistics
Incoterms 2020 — Complete Reference Guide
Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) are a globally recognised set of 11 rules published by the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC) that define who is responsible for shipping, insurance, import/export duties, and risk at every point of a shipment's journey. Getting Incoterms wrong is one of the most expensive mistakes in international logistics.
Group 1 — Any Mode
EXW — Ex Works
Seller's obligation ends at their factory gate. Buyer arranges and pays for everything — loading, transport, insurance, customs.
  • Risk transfers: When goods are at seller's premises
  • Best for: Buyer has strong logistics capability
  • Avoid if: You don't have local agents in seller's country
Real ExampleYou buy from a factory in China on EXW terms. You are responsible for trucking goods from the factory to the port, export customs, sea freight, import customs, and delivery to your warehouse.
Group 2 — Any Mode
DAP / DDP — Delivered at Place / Duty Paid
DAP: Seller delivers to named destination. Buyer handles import duties only.
DDP: Seller handles everything including import duties. Maximum obligation on seller.
  • Risk transfers (DAP): At named destination, before unloading
  • Best for: Buyers with limited logistics infrastructure
Real ExampleA supplier offers DDP to your warehouse. You pay a higher unit price but have zero logistics responsibility. Simple — but you lose visibility and control of the supply chain.
Group 3 — Sea Only
FOB — Free On Board
One of the most commonly used terms. Seller's responsibility ends when goods are loaded on the vessel at the origin port. Buyer arranges and pays for sea freight, insurance, and onward delivery.
  • Risk transfers: When goods pass the ship's rail at port of origin
  • Best for: When buyer wants to control freight cost and carrier selection
Real ExampleYou buy 500 units FOB Shanghai. Seller trucks goods to Shanghai port and loads them. From that point, your freight forwarder handles the rest. Common in textile, electronics, and consumer goods procurement.
Group 4 — Sea Only
CIF — Cost, Insurance & Freight
Seller pays for shipping and minimum insurance to the destination port. Risk still transfers to buyer once goods are loaded at origin port — despite seller paying freight.
  • Risk transfers: At origin port (same as FOB) — but seller pays freight
  • Watch out: Insurance is minimum — buyer should top up
Real ExampleCIF Mumbai means your supplier pays freight and basic insurance to Mumbai port. But if goods are damaged in transit, you bear the loss — and the minimum insurance may not cover full value. Always add your own insurance on CIF shipments.
Group 5 — Any Mode
FCA — Free Carrier
Seller delivers goods to a named carrier or location chosen by the buyer. Replaces EXW in most modern containerised trade — more practical than EXW for container shipments.
  • Risk transfers: When goods are handed to buyer's carrier
  • Best for: Containerised freight where buyer controls the carrier
Real ExampleFCA at seller's warehouse, Guangzhou. The seller loads the container at their site and hands it to your nominated forwarder. Risk and cost transfer at that point.
Quick Reference
Incoterms: Who Pays What?
Increasing buyer responsibility →

EXW → FCA → FOB → CFR → CIF → DAP → DDP

Increasing seller responsibility →

Rule of thumb:
  • EXW/FCA = Buyer controls logistics
  • FOB/CIF = Shared responsibility
  • DAP/DDP = Seller controls logistics
Key RuleAlways specify Incoterms with a named place: "FOB Shanghai Port 2020" — not just "FOB." The named place determines exactly where risk transfers.
🎯 Key Takeaway for Your Team Incoterms directly impact your landed cost, your risk exposure, and your insurance requirements. Never accept an Incoterm without calculating its total impact. FOB and CIF are the most common in international procurement — master these two first. Always state the year (2020) and named location in every contract and PO. When in doubt, choose the Incoterm that gives you control over your freight — because that control gives you cost visibility.

Store & Inventory Management

📘 Deep Dive · Week 1 · Store & Inventory
ABC Analysis — The Foundation of Inventory Control
ABC Analysis is a method of classifying inventory items into three categories based on their value and usage frequency. It comes from the Pareto Principle (80/20 rule) — the idea that roughly 80% of your inventory value comes from just 20% of your items. By knowing which items are A, B, or C, you know exactly where to focus your time, money, and attention.
Category A
High Value · Tight Control
Typically: Top 10–20% of SKUs that account for 70–80% of total inventory value.

Management approach:
  • Weekly stock reviews — no exceptions
  • Tight safety stock — calculated precisely, not estimated
  • Minimum 2 qualified suppliers always available
  • Real-time tracking preferred (RFID or bin-level monitoring)
  • Senior team member responsible for each A-item
ExampleIn a manufacturing store: motors, control panels, or any item that stops production if out of stock. In a retail store: your top 10 best-selling SKUs by revenue.
Category B
Medium Value · Periodic Review
Typically: Next 30–40% of SKUs that account for 15–25% of total inventory value.

Management approach:
  • Monthly stock reviews — scheduled, not ad-hoc
  • Standard safety stock based on lead time formula
  • One primary + one identified backup supplier
  • Barcode-based tracking is sufficient
  • Review quarterly whether items should move to A or C
ExampleMid-range consumables, components that are used regularly but are not business-critical. Important enough to track — not important enough to obsess over daily.
Category C
Low Value · Simplified Management
Typically: Remaining 40–50% of SKUs that account for only 5–10% of total inventory value.

Management approach:
  • Quarterly or manual reviews only
  • Higher safety stock acceptable — carrying cost is low
  • Bulk ordering to minimise ordering frequency
  • Simple min/max system sufficient
  • Consider consignment stock arrangements with suppliers
ExampleStationery, packaging materials, low-cost fasteners, general consumables. Don't spend A-item energy on C-item management.
How to Do It
Step-by-Step: Building Your ABC Classification
Step 1: List all SKUs with annual usage quantity
Step 2: Multiply quantity × unit cost = Annual Value per SKU
Step 3: Sort all SKUs from highest to lowest annual value
Step 4: Calculate cumulative % of total value
Step 5: Label top 70–80% cumulative value = A; next 15–25% = B; remaining = C

Rule of ThumbIf you have 500 SKUs: expect roughly 50–100 to be A-class, 150–200 to be B-class, and 200–300 to be C-class. Review this classification every 6 months — items move between categories as demand patterns change.
Common Mistake
ABC by Value Only Is Incomplete — Add Criticality
A low-value item can still be business-critical. For example, a $2 O-ring that stops a $2 million machine from running must be treated as an A-item despite its low cost.

Best practice: Use ABC-XYZ analysis:
  • XYZ classifies by demand stability: X = stable, Y = variable, Z = unpredictable
  • AX = highest priority: high value + stable demand = easiest to manage
  • CZ = lowest priority but watch for criticality
Criticality OverrideAny item that causes a production stoppage or a direct customer impact if unavailable should be treated as A-class regardless of its unit cost.
Reorder Formula
Applying ABC to Your Reorder Points
Reorder Point = (Avg Daily Usage × Lead Time) + Safety Stock

A-items: Calculate precisely. Review monthly. Safety stock = 1–2 weeks of usage.
B-items: Calculate quarterly. Safety stock = 2–4 weeks.
C-items: Set a generous minimum and reorder in bulk. Safety stock = 4–8 weeks or more.

Example — A-itemDaily usage: 10 units. Lead time: 7 days. Safety stock: 30 units. Reorder Point = (10 × 7) + 30 = 100 units. When stock hits 100, raise the order.
🎯 Key Takeaway for Your Team ABC Analysis is not a one-time exercise — it is a living classification that should be reviewed every 6 months. The power is in the discipline it creates: spend your energy managing A-items intensively and automate or simplify C-item management. Most store teams make the mistake of treating all items equally — this wastes time on low-value items while A-items go unmonitored. Run your ABC classification this month and make it visible to the whole team.

🌐 Global Industry Insight
Cycle Counting Is Replacing Annual Stock Takes in Leading Warehouses
Globally, high-performing warehouse operations have largely abandoned the once-a-year "wall-to-wall" stock take in favour of continuous cycle counting — counting a small portion of inventory every day or week so that every item is counted multiple times per year.

Why leading warehouses prefer cycle counting:
  • Discrepancies are found and fixed immediately — not discovered 11 months later
  • No need to shut down operations for a full stock count
  • Team builds accuracy habits through regular practice rather than annual stress
  • Inventory records are trusted in real time — enabling better ordering decisions
Amazon's fulfilment centres run continuous cycle counts on A-items daily, with the entire inventory counted at least once per quarter — a practice now spreading to mid-size operations globally.
Source: Logimax WMS Industry Report 2026 · Kardex Warehouse Trends · Modula Best Practices
📌 What This Means for Your Team This Week
  • Choose one zone of your store and run a cycle count today. Compare to system records. Document the accuracy %.
  • If you don't have a cycle counting schedule, create one — even a basic one covering your A-items weekly.
  • Calculate your current inventory accuracy rate: (Correct records ÷ Total items counted) × 100. Share the result with your team.

Behaviour & Interpersonal Skills

📘 Deep Dive · Week 1 · Behaviour & Skills
Emotional Intelligence (EQ) — The Complete Breakdown
Emotional Intelligence (EQ) is the ability to recognise, understand, manage, and effectively use your own emotions and the emotions of others. Research by Dr. Daniel Goleman shows EQ accounts for nearly 90% of what separates high performers from average performers in leadership roles — more than IQ or technical skill.
EQ Pillar 01
Self-Awareness
The ability to accurately recognise your own emotions and how they affect your thoughts and behaviour.

Signs of high self-awareness:
  • You know your emotional triggers before they control you
  • You can name what you're feeling accurately
  • You understand your strengths and genuine weaknesses
  • You seek feedback — and hear it without becoming defensive
This Week's PracticeAt the end of each day, take 2 minutes to name one emotion you felt strongly during the day and what triggered it. Patterns reveal your emotional landscape.
EQ Pillar 02
Self-Management
The ability to control disruptive emotions and impulses and to adapt to changing circumstances.

What it looks like in practice:
  • Pausing before reacting to frustration or provocation
  • Staying calm and focused under deadline pressure
  • Following through on commitments even when motivation drops
  • Adapting your approach when a situation changes unexpectedly
This Week's PracticeThe next time you feel a strong negative emotion at work, try the 6-second rule: pause for 6 seconds before saying or doing anything. This is the time it takes for the emotional brain to allow the rational brain to take over.
EQ Pillar 03
Social Awareness (Empathy)
The ability to accurately sense what others are feeling and to understand their perspective, even when they don't express it directly.

What it looks like in practice:
  • Noticing when a team member is stressed before they tell you
  • Listening to understand — not just to respond
  • Adjusting your communication style based on who you're speaking to
  • Being curious about others' viewpoints before sharing your own
This Week's PracticeIn your next team interaction, focus entirely on listening. Don't plan your response while they're talking. After they finish, ask one follow-up question before you speak. Notice what you learn that you would have missed otherwise.
EQ Pillar 04
Relationship Management
The ability to influence, coach, mentor, and inspire others — and to manage conflict constructively.

What it looks like in practice:
  • Giving feedback that improves performance without damaging relationships
  • Resolving team conflicts before they escalate
  • Inspiring others during uncertainty or pressure
  • Building trust consistently through actions, not just words
This Week's PracticeIdentify one person on your team whose potential you haven't fully acknowledged recently. Have a 5-minute genuine conversation with them this week — not about tasks, but about how they are finding their work and what would make it better.
EQ vs IQ
Why EQ Outperforms IQ in Leadership
Research across 500,000 professionals by TalentSmart found:
  • EQ is the single biggest predictor of performance — explaining 58% of job success
  • 90% of top performers score high in EQ
  • Only 20% of bottom performers score high in EQ
  • Professionals with high EQ earn an average of $29,000 more per year
Key InsightIQ gets you the job. EQ gets you promoted. Technical skill makes you competent. Emotional intelligence makes you someone people trust, follow, and want to work with.
For Your Team
Building EQ in a Supply Chain Environment
Supply chain and procurement roles are high-pressure — tight deadlines, supplier conflicts, internal stakeholder tension. EQ is your toolkit for navigating all of this.

Three habits that build EQ:
  • Daily reflection: 5 minutes to review your emotional reactions that day
  • Feedback seeking: Ask one colleague weekly: "What's one thing I could do differently?"
  • Perspective-taking: Before any difficult conversation, spend 2 minutes thinking from the other person's viewpoint
🎯 Key Takeaway for Your Team EQ is not a personality trait you either have or don't have — it is a skill that is built through deliberate practice. The four pillars are a development roadmap: start with self-awareness (knowing your own emotions), move to self-management (controlling them), build social awareness (sensing others'), and develop relationship management (using all of the above to work better with people). Pick one pillar to work on this week and one practice habit from above. Small, consistent actions compound into a meaningfully different way of showing up at work.

📘 Deep Dive · Week 1 · AI & Technology
What is AI? — A Practical Guide for Supply Chain Professionals
Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to computer systems that can perform tasks that typically require human intelligence — such as pattern recognition, prediction, decision-making, and language understanding. For supply chain professionals, AI is not about robots replacing people. It is about giving your team superhuman data-processing capabilities so you can make better decisions, faster.
AI Type 01
Machine Learning (ML)
Systems that learn from data and improve their predictions over time without being explicitly programmed for each scenario.

In supply chain, ML powers:
  • Demand forecasting — predicting what to order and when
  • Price prediction — anticipating when commodity prices will spike
  • Supplier risk scoring — identifying which suppliers are likely to fail before they do
Real ApplicationAmazon's replenishment system uses ML to predict what you will order before you order it — pre-positioning stock in the nearest fulfilment centre. This reduces delivery time and cost simultaneously.
AI Type 02
Natural Language Processing (NLP)
AI that can read, understand, and generate human language — text and speech.

In supply chain, NLP powers:
  • Contract analysis — reading and flagging risky clauses instantly
  • Email classification — categorising and routing supplier communications
  • Chatbots — handling standard supplier or internal queries 24/7
  • Document processing — extracting data from invoices, POs, and shipping documents
Real ApplicationTools like Claude, ChatGPT, and Copilot are NLP-powered. Right now, your team can use them to draft RFQs, summarise contracts, and write professional supplier emails in seconds.
AI Type 03
Computer Vision
AI that can interpret and analyse images and video in real time.

In supply chain, Computer Vision powers:
  • Quality inspection — detecting defects on production lines automatically
  • Warehouse picking verification — confirming correct items are picked
  • Inventory counting — using cameras to count stock without manual scanning
  • Vehicle safety monitoring — tracking driver behaviour in logistics fleets
Real ApplicationFoxconn (Apple's manufacturer) uses computer vision to inspect 60 million components per day — catching defects that human inspectors would miss due to fatigue.
AI Type 04
Agentic AI (Autonomous AI)
AI systems that can take actions and make decisions autonomously within defined boundaries — not just provide recommendations.

In supply chain, Agentic AI can:
  • Automatically raise purchase orders when stock hits reorder point
  • Reroute shipments when port congestion is detected
  • Request quotes from approved suppliers and rank responses
  • Flag contract compliance deviations without human trigger
2026 RealityThis is no longer experimental. Multiple Fortune 500 companies now run fully autonomous procurement cycles for C-category items — freeing procurement teams to focus on strategic A-item decisions.
Start Here
What Your Team Can Do With AI Today — For Free
You do not need enterprise software to start benefiting from AI. Right now, using free tools:
  • Draft supplier emails — describe the situation, ask AI to write the email
  • Summarise long reports — paste text, ask for a 5-point summary
  • Generate comparison tables — list suppliers and criteria, get a structured table in seconds
  • Create first-draft RFQs — describe your requirement, get a professional document
  • Research market prices — ask for current benchmark pricing for any commodity
The Skill
Prompt Engineering: How to Talk to AI Effectively
The quality of AI output depends entirely on the quality of your instruction (prompt). Poor prompt = poor result. Strong prompt = exceptional result.

Formula for a strong prompt:
Role + Context + Task + Format + Constraints

Example"You are a procurement expert. I am sourcing 5,000 units of industrial gloves from a new supplier in China. Draft a professional RFQ email covering: specifications, quantity, delivery terms (FOB), payment terms, sample request, and timeline. Keep it under 300 words and use formal business English."

The teams that will thrive are those that develop AI literacy now, before it becomes mandatory. You don't need to understand the code. You need to understand what each type of AI can do, which problems it solves, and how to direct it effectively. Start this week: use one AI tool for one real work task. Build the habit before you need to build the skill under pressure.
